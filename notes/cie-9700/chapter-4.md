# Chapter 4: Cell Membranes and Transport

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Describe the structure of cell membranes (fluid mosaic model)
2. Explain the roles of membrane components
3. Distinguish between different types of membrane transport
4. Understand osmosis and water potential
5. Explain active transport and cotransport

## Key Concepts

### Fluid Mosaic Model

The cell membrane consists of:
- **Phospholipid bilayer:** Forms the basic structure
- **Proteins:** Embedded or attached to the bilayer
- **Cholesterol:** Regulates fluidity
- **Carbohydrates:** Attached to proteins/lipids (glycoproteins/glycolipids)

### Membrane Components

#### Phospholipids
- Amphipathic molecules (hydrophilic head, hydrophobic tails)
- Form bilayer with hydrophobic tails facing inward
- Heads face aqueous environments

#### Membrane Proteins

| Type | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| Integral | Span entire membrane | Channel proteins, carrier proteins |
| Peripheral | Attached to surface | Enzymes, recognition proteins |

#### Cholesterol
- Found in animal cell membranes
- Fits between phospholipids
- Prevents tight packing (maintains fluidity)
- Reduces permeability to small water-soluble molecules

## Important Formulas

### Water Potential Equation

$$\Psi = \Psi_s + \Psi_p$$

Where:
- $\Psi$ = water potential (measured in kPa or MPa)
- $\Psi_s$ = solute potential (always negative or zero)
- $\Psi_p$ = pressure potential (usually positive)

### Osmotic Relationships

| Solution | Water Potential | Solute Concentration |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Pure water | 0 (highest) | Zero |
| Dilute solution | Negative | Low |
| Concentrated solution | More negative | High |

> **Key principle:** Water moves from high water potential to low water potential (from less negative to more negative).

## Types of Membrane Transport

### Passive Transport

No energy (ATP) required - movement down concentration gradient.

#### Simple Diffusion
- Movement of small, non-polar molecules
- Directly through phospholipid bilayer
- Examples: O₂, CO₂, fatty acids, steroids

#### Facilitated Diffusion
- Movement through protein channels or carriers
- Still passive (no energy required)
- Examples: Glucose, amino acids, ions

| Feature | Channel Proteins | Carrier Proteins |
|---------|------------------|------------------|
| Structure | Pore through membrane | Bind and change shape |
| Specificity | Less specific | More specific |
| Rate | Faster | Slower |
| Gating | Can be gated | No gating |

#### Osmosis
- Diffusion of water across a partially permeable membrane
- From high water potential to low water potential
- Depends on solute concentration

### Active Transport

Requires energy (ATP) - movement against concentration gradient.

#### Features:
- Uses carrier proteins (pumps)
- Requires ATP hydrolysis
- Can move substances against gradient
- Examples: Na⁺/K⁺ pump, proton pumps

#### Sodium-Potassium Pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase)

$$3\text{Na}^+ \text{(out)} + 2\text{K}^+ \text{(in)} + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{ADP} + P_i$$

- Pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in
- Maintains electrochemical gradient
- Important for nerve impulse transmission

### Comparison of Transport Mechanisms

| Feature | Simple Diffusion | Facilitated Diffusion | Active Transport |
|---------|------------------|----------------------|------------------|
| Energy required | No | No | Yes (ATP) |
| Against gradient | No | No | Yes |
| Protein required | No | Yes | Yes |
| Rate | Slow | Faster | Variable |
| Saturable | No | Yes | Yes |
| Specificity | Low | High | High |

## Bulk Transport

### Endocytosis
- Movement of materials INTO cell
- Membrane invaginates and forms vesicle
- Types:
  - **Phagocytosis:** Solid particles ("cell eating")
  - **Pinocytosis:** Liquid ("cell drinking")
  - **Receptor-mediated:** Specific molecules bind to receptors

### Exocytosis
- Movement of materials OUT of cell
- Vesicle fuses with plasma membrane
- Used for secretion (hormones, neurotransmitters)

## Transport in Plant Cells

### Plasmolysis
- Cell loses water to hypertonic solution
- Cell membrane pulls away from cell wall
- Cytoplasm shrinks

### Turgidity
- Cell gains water in hypotonic solution
- Cell membrane pushes against cell wall
- Cell becomes firm (turgid)

### Water Potential in Plants

| Condition | $\Psi_s$ | $\Psi_p$ | $\Psi$ | Result |
|-----------|----------|----------|---------|--------|
| Pure water | 0 | 0 | 0 | Reference |
| Flaccid cell | Negative | 0 | Negative | No net movement |
| Turgid cell | Negative | Positive | Less negative | Water enters |
| Plasmolyzed | More negative | 0 | More negative | Water leaves |

## Exam Tips

1. **Fluid mosaic:** Remember the membrane is dynamic, not static
2. **Water potential:** Always moves from high (less negative) to low (more negative)
3. **Transport types:** Be clear on the differences between diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport
4. **Protein types:** Know the difference between channel and carrier proteins
5. **Plant cells:** Understand plasmolysis and turgidity in terms of water potential

## Practice Questions

1. Explain why the cell membrane is described as "fluid" and "mosaic."

2. Compare and contrast facilitated diffusion and active transport.

3. A cell is placed in a solution with water potential -200 kPa. The cell's water potential is -100 kPa. Predict the direction of water movement and explain your answer.

4. Describe the role of the sodium-potassium pump in maintaining the resting potential of a neuron.
