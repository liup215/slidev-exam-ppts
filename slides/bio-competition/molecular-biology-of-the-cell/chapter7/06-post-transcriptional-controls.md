## Transcription Attenuation and Riboswitches

### Transcription Attenuation
- **Premature termination**: RNA polymerase stops before completing full transcript
- **HIV Tat protein**: Binds TAR stem-loop structure in nascent RNA, prevents premature termination
- **Viral hijacking**: HIV uses normal cellular mechanism to control its own transcription

### Riboswitches
- **Ancient control devices**: RNA structures that change conformation when binding small molecules
- **Metabolite sensing**: Common in bacteria for purine biosynthesis, vitamin metabolism, etc.
- **Economical regulation**: Bypass need for regulatory proteins entirely
- **Multiple mechanisms**: Can control transcription elongation, translation initiation, or RNA stability

> **Evolutionary significance**: Supports "RNA world" hypothesis - RNA can store information, catalyze reactions, AND control gene expression

## Alternative RNA Splicing

### Splicing Diversity
- **Protein variants**: Single gene produces multiple protein isoforms through different splicing patterns
- **Drosophila Dscam**: Extreme example - single gene can produce 38,016 different proteins
- **Constitutive vs regulated**: Some alternative splicing occurs by chance; other is tightly controlled

### Regulatory Mechanisms
- **Negative control**: Repressor proteins block splice sites, forcing use of alternative sites
- **Positive control**: Activator proteins help splicing machinery recognize weak splice sites
- **Splicing enhancers**: RNA sequences (often distant from splice sites) that promote splicing

### Biological Examples
- **Drosophila P element**: Functional transposase in germ cells, nonfunctional in somatic cells
- **Tropomyosin**: Different isoforms in different cell types for specialized functions
- **Gene definition evolution**: Original "one gene, one protein" concept modified due to alternative splicing

## Back Splicing and Circular RNAs

### Back Splicing Mechanism
- **Reverse joining**: 3' splice site joined to downstream 5' splice site
- **Circular RNA formation**: Single exon released as covalently closed circular molecule
- **Stability advantage**: No free ends = resistant to normal RNA degradation enzymes

### Circular RNA Functions
- **Molecular sponges**: Soak up complementary RNAs and RNA-binding proteins
- **Scaffolds**: Provide platforms for multisubunit RNA-protein complexes
- **Accumulation**: Stable nature allows high concentrations in specific tissues (brain, immune system)

## Alternative Polyadenylation

### C-terminal Variation
- **Cleavage site selection**: Different poly-A addition sites create proteins with different C-termini
- **B lymphocyte example**: Membrane-bound vs secreted antibody forms
  - **Membrane form**: Long hydrophobic C-terminus anchors in plasma membrane
  - **Secreted form**: Short hydrophilic C-terminus allows release from cell

### Regulation Mechanism
- **CstF concentration**: Increased CstF levels promote cleavage at suboptimal upstream sites
- **Antigen stimulation**: Activated B cells produce more CstF, switching to secreted antibody production

## RNA Modifications and Editing

### mRNA Base Modifications
- **N6-methyladenosine (m6A)**: Most common modification (~1-3 per mRNA)
- **Dynamic regulation**: Constantly added by methylases, removed by demethylases ("erasers")
- **Functional effects**:
  - Destabilizes hairpin structures, altering RNA secondary structure
  - Promotes mRNA degradation through "reader" proteins
  - Enhances translation through reader proteins that recruit translation machinery

### RNA Editing
- **A-to-I editing**: Adenosine deaminated to inosine (pairs like guanine)
  - **Brain ion channels**: Single edit changes glutamine to arginine, alters Ca2+ permeability
  - **ADAR enzymes**: Recognize double-stranded RNA structures formed by intron-exon pairing
- **C-to-U editing**: Cytosine deaminated to uracil
  - **Apolipoprotein B**: Creates premature stop codon in intestine, producing truncated protein vs full-length liver version

> **Evolutionary theories**: May correct genomic "mistakes," provide protein diversity, or evolved from antiviral defense mechanisms

## RNA Transport and Localization

### Nuclear Export Regulation
- **HIV Rev protein**: Binds Rev Response Element (RRE) in viral intron, overcomes nuclear retention of unspliced RNAs
- **Temporal control**: Early phase (fully spliced RNAs only) → Late phase (unspliced RNAs exported)
- **Viral latency**: Low Rev/Tat levels stall viral growth until conditions improve

### mRNA Localization
- **Spatial targeting**: mRNAs directed to specific cytoplasmic locations before translation
- **3' UTR signals**: Act as "ZIP codes" for intracellular targeting
- **Biological examples**:
  - **Drosophila Bicoid**: mRNA localized to anterior egg tip, creates protein gradient for development
  - **Mammalian actin**: mRNA localized to actin-rich cell cortex via 3' UTR signal
- **Translational coupling**: Localized mRNAs often translationally repressed until properly positioned

## Translational Control

### Initiation Regulation
- **Bacterial control**: Shine-Dalgarno sequence accessibility (blocked by repressors, antisense RNA, or structural changes)
- **Eukaryotic control**: 5' cap accessibility, upstream open reading frames (uORFs), internal ribosome entry sites (IRES)

### Global Translation Control
- **eIF2 phosphorylation**: Stress, starvation, or viral infection → eIF2-P sequesters eIF2B → global translation reduction
- **Selective enhancement**: Paradoxically increases translation of specific mRNAs (e.g., Gcn4 in yeast during amino acid starvation)

### Specialized Mechanisms
- **Leaky scanning**: Ribosomes skip suboptimal first AUG, initiate at downstream AUG
- **IRES elements**: Allow cap-independent translation initiation, commonly used by viruses to hijack host machinery

## mRNA Stability and Degradation

### Decay Pathways
- **Poly-A shortening**: Gradual deadenylation acts as "timer" for mRNA lifetime
- **Two convergence points**:
  1. **Decapping**: 5' cap removal → rapid 5'-to-3' degradation
  2. **3'-to-5' degradation**: Continued exonuclease activity through coding sequence

### Regulated Stability
- **Iron regulation example**:
  - **Transferrin receptor mRNA**: Iron starvation → aconitase binds 3' UTR → blocks endonucleolytic cleavage → mRNA stabilized
  - **Ferritin mRNA**: Iron starvation → aconitase binds 5' UTR → blocks translation initiation
- **3' UTR importance**: Contains binding sites for proteins that regulate poly-A shortening, decapping, or degradation rates

### P-bodies and Stress Granules
- **P-bodies**: Contain mRNA degradation enzymes; can also store translationally repressed mRNAs for later reactivation
- **Stress granules**: Form during translation inhibition; store ribosome-free mRNAs until stress resolves
- **Dynamic cycling**: mRNAs can move between active translation, storage, and degradation pathways based on cellular needs

> **Key principle**: mRNA fate is carefully controlled - cells invest heavily in mRNA production and manage subsequent steps precisely
